Technique

An interview is essentially a structured conversation where one participant asks questions, and the other provides answers. The traditional two-person interview format permits direct questions and follow-ups, which enables an interviewer to better gauge the accuracy of responses. It is a flexible arrangement in the sense that subsequent questions can be tailored to clarify earlier answers.[1]

 

Since 70 something percent of communication is non-verbal, it is critical that meeting your client in person happens to ensure you can communicate clearly with them. With so many chances for misunderstanding each other, especially in the world of software development, it is important to mitigate the chances that miscommunication can happen, and interviewing meets that criteria. Along with that is the ability to read emotions right there on the spot so the interviewer can use live thinking to help meet the interviewee in the middle. It is also a lot easier to keep focus when speaking with another human being as opposed to reading a survey document or filling out a questionnaire to gauge what type of project is underway.

 

These are all great advantages, but almost all of them become void if the interviewer hired does not know what they are doing, what they are asking, or how to interact with humans. If they cannot read those social queues, or gauge the interviewee’s emotions, then it can actually do more harm than good and lose the team the job. This is the biggest downfall to interviewing, as the cost can be manageable, and a professional should be able to manually enter data correctly. Another dangerous aspect of interviews is biasing on either side. There are techniques to be protected from bias, but we will not discuss them here.[2][3]

 

There are several things to bring into consideration when preparing to conduct an interview. They include but are not limited to [4][5]:

·         Researching to come up with questions

·         Writing a script of questions that you need answered with possible follow-up questions and using it throughout the interview

·         Ordering questions so that easier questions are asked first and more difficult questions later in the interview

·         Avoiding questions that force a specific answer, or having simple yes-no answers

·         Practicing before conducting the actual interview

·         Preparing a way to record or transcribe the interview, to allow for ease of access to the information

·         Establishing and Maintaining a Relationship with the Client or Interviewee

·         Matching the Language of the Interviewee

·         Including Expanded or Larger Systems in the Interview

·         Using a Team Process Effectively

·         Ending and Summarizing the Interview Process

·         If needed and or possible, set up a second interview for after the transcription of the first interview, to allow for clarifications or further questions
