Overview of the Technique

This report details the process of the requirements workshop and the planning and results of the workshop. The report will focus on the overview of the technique, planning, raw data, and concluding information on the requirements workshop. This report is meant to further explain the requirements workshop elicitation technique, and the results that were gathered by employing this technique.

The requirements workshop is meant to provide a channel for both the sponsor and the stakeholders to all meet and develop requirements for the project to be created. According to Didar Zowghi, Associate Professor of Software Engineering, and Chad Coulin, member of the Requirements Engineering research group in the Faculty of Information Technology at the University of Technology, Sydney, “the emphasis is on developing and discovering requirements for a software system [1].” Requirement workshops are meant to increase communication and collaboration among the stakeholders of the project. The workshops held are the beginning of a creative process with the goal of producing new applications.

The elicitation technique workshop is one of the many elicitation techniques used to build and implement requirements. Workshop is convenient when distinguish stakeholder groups are enrolled in the project. The sessions are conducted by a facilitator and attended by project representatives and specialists from each one of the involved groups who plan meetings to discuss and to work on the implementation of project requirements. Neil Maiden, who is Professor of Digital Creativity in the Faculty of Management at the Cass Business and co-founder of the Centre for Creativity in Professional Practice at City University London, said that “One of the obvious advantages of using group workshops is the ability to integrate other elicitation techniques into them, and then to incorporate their combined usage into a deﬁned requirements process [2].”

From the workshop, several requirements were defined. Speaking for the Activities App as a whole, the sponsor stated that the app should be available free of charge. To begin with, these are some of the core requirements for the app as a whole:

  • App is free to download and create an account
  • App is offered only through iOS (initially)
  • Target users will be residents and students in Rexburg, Idaho (initially)
  • Target users are young adults (ages 18-26), but other age groups may also use the app

The workshop also discovered that the Activities App will have two classes of users, those representing local businesses and those who represent only themselves as people seeking to participate in activities. As the data is presented, these will be referred to as “businesses” and “personal users” respectively. Each of their roles are outlined as follows:

Businesses

  • Able to create activities to take place at their place of business.
  • Pay to have their activities promoted. Highest-bidding business has activity listed at the top of the list and so forth.

Personal Users

  • Never charged money by the app
  • Upon account creation, must supply gender, age, etc.
    • Can also sign up through Facebook, Google, etc. if desired
  • Able to browse a list of activities presented by businesses
  • Able to browse a list of events created by personal users seeking other personal users to join their events
    • A “Random” button is also present. Upon clicking this button, the personal user would be presented with a randomly selected event where the event creator is seeking participants.
  • Able to create an event (events are different from activities, which are created by businesses)
    • Event will be linked to a business’s activity
    • Able to select the desired number of participants, which will cause the event to be viewable by other personal users
    • Able to select that the event is a “date” (which would require one additional participant of the opposite gender)
    • After event creation, able to reject requests by other personal users to join the event
  • Able to interact with other personal users
    • Able to add other personal users to a network of friends (it is unknown what these will eventually be referred to as, but here they will be referred to as “network friends”); this can be done after participating in an event with the other personal user
    • Able to send direct messages to network friends
    • Able to report a personal user who has bailed on (i.e. didn’t show up to) an event they had signed up to attend

The sponsor also had several ideas about how requirements might change in the future of the app (if it does well when implementing its initial requirements). These are not current requirements, but they may be useful to consider while architecting the app initially.

  • App would potentially be available on platforms other than iOS (e.g. Android)
  • Instead of targeting users only in the Rexburg area, app would target users in a wider geographical range
  • Geographic proximity to personal user would be considered when showing available activities and events. E.g. a Salt Lake City user may not want to see an event happening in Rexburg.
  • Personal user would be able to invite network friends to the personal user’s created events
  • Geographic proximity on a small scale (even to how many feet away another personal user is) would allow users to be made aware of events currently happening nearby or when other personal users are nearby. This might be done either through push notifications, or just when the app is open and/or this information is requested.

Conclusion

This report has detailed the process of the requirements workshop, the plan that was created before the requirement workshop occurred, and the results of the performed elicitation technique. The requirement workshop has helped to provide requirements for the application that will help contribute to other documents that will be made for this application.

The requirements workshop also helped to create a channel of communication with the sponsor; this is a channel of communication that must be maintained for the duration of the project. Further requirements workshops should be performed many more times as needed to ensure the application has all the components it needs to be a success.
